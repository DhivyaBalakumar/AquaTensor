Methodology

Data Sources & Calculation Basis

Every constant and formula in this simulator is grounded in published industry data, peer-reviewed research, or established engineering benchmarks. This section documents the logic and references behind each number so results can be explained, challenged, and refined.

Formula 1
Total Heat Available
Total Heat (MW) = IT Load × 0.8
Basis: Nearly 100% of electricity consumed by IT equipment is ultimately dissipated as heat — servers, storage, and networking convert virtually all electrical energy into thermal energy. The 0.8 factor (80%) is a deliberate conservative assumption that accounts for heat escaping before capture, transmission losses in the cooling loop, and variability across equipment generations.

The theoretical maximum is closer to 0.97–1.0, but using 0.8 ensures the simulator does not overstate recovery potential.
1
EUDCA Technical Committee White Paper (2024) — "Nearly 100% of the electricity consumed by IT equipment is dissipated as waste heat." theenergyst.com
2
Verne Global (2025) — "Nearly 97% of the electrical energy consumed by data centers could be harnessed in the form of heat." verneglobal.com
Solid — conservative by design
Formula 2
Waste Heat Available (20–50%)
Usable Heat (MW) = Total Heat × Waste Heat %
Basis: Not all waste heat is at a temperature or location suitable for recovery. The practical capture range depends on cooling architecture:

— Air-cooled facilities: 20–30% of heat is at a useful temperature for recovery systems
— Liquid-cooled / rear-door: 35–50% is recoverable at useful temperatures
— Immersion cooling: up to 50%+ is directly recoverable

Theoretical models show 80%+ recovery is possible, but practical on-site infrastructure constraints limit real deployments to this range.
3
Araner (2024) — "A large share (often above 80%) of electricity consumption can theoretically be recovered as heat" — but practical capture is constrained by temperature and infrastructure. araner.com
4
ResearchGate — A review of data center cooling technology and low-grade waste heat recovery opportunities. Identifies 20–50% as the practical capture range across cooling types. researchgate.net
Solid — matches documented deployment ranges
Formula 3
Recoverable Heat Efficiency (30–70%)
Recovered Heat (MW) = Usable Heat × Recovery Efficiency %
Basis: Thermal recovery systems — heat exchangers, absorption chillers, and organic Rankine cycle (ORC) units — operate at 30–70% efficiency in documented real-world deployments. The default of 50% represents a mid-range system performing as designed under normal operating conditions.

Absorption cooling and ORC are identified as the most promising technologies for data center waste heat reuse, with efficiency varying by temperature differential and system design.
5
ResearchGate — Waste Heat Recoveries in Data Centers: A Review (2023). Documents 30–70% efficiency range across heat exchanger and absorption chiller deployments. researchgate.net
6
ResearchGate — A review of data center cooling technology and low-grade waste heat recovery. Identifies absorption cooling and ORC as most promising, with efficiency 30–65%. researchgate.net
Solid — consistent with published system performance
Formula 4
Water Production
Water Produced (L/day) = Recovered Heat (MW) × 15,000
Basis: This is a conservative approximation for low-grade thermal water production via waste-heat-driven condensation or distillation processes. To contextualise the figure:

— Reverse osmosis desalination requires ~0.83–4.5 kWh per m³ of water
— 1 MW thermal over 24 hours = 24,000 kWh/day
— At 4.5 kWh/m³ (worst-case RO): 24,000 ÷ 4.5 = ~5,333 m³/day = 5.3 million L/day theoretical max
— The 15,000 L/MW/day figure is ~0.3% of that theoretical maximum

This extreme conservatism is intentional: it accounts for low-grade heat limitations, system losses, and the fact that data center waste heat is typically below 60°C — less efficient than high-temperature industrial processes. Real deployments should exceed this floor.
7
WaterOnline — Waste Heat Recovery for Desalination from Steam Power Plants. RO desalination energy: ~4.5 kWh per 1,000 litres. wateronline.com
8
ResearchGate — A review on energy consumption of desalination processes. Benchmarks thermal and membrane desalination energy requirements. researchgate.net
Conservative floor — real output likely higher
Formula 5
Baseline Water Consumption
Water Consumed (L/day) = IT Load (MW) × 50,000
Basis: Derived from the industry-standard WUE (Water Usage Effectiveness) metric — the ratio of annual site water usage (litres) to IT energy consumed (kWh):

— Industry average WUE ≈ 1.8 L/kWh (US average, LBNL / Scope3 Methodology)
— 1 MW IT load = 1,000 kW
— 1,000 kW × 24 hours × 1.8 L/kWh = 43,200 L/day
— Rounded up to 50,000 L/MW/day to account for India's warmer climate, which increases evaporative cooling demand and raises effective WUE

This field is user-editable. Operators with known WUE data should override this with their actual consumption figures.
9
Data Center Knowledge (2025) — Industry average WUE ~1.8 L/kWh. datacenterknowledge.com
10
Scope3 Methodology — "The US average WUE according to United States Data Center Energy Usage Report (2016) is 1.8 L/kWh." scope3.com
11
Nature — Data centre water consumption (2021). Peer-reviewed analysis of global data center water use patterns. nature.com
Solid — WUE-derived, climate-adjusted
Formula 6
Cooling Load & Energy Savings
Cooling Load (MW) = IT Load × 0.3 Energy Savings (MW) = Cooling Load × 0.3
Basis: Cooling systems account for 30–40% of total data center energy consumption, consistently cited across multiple independent industry sources. Using 30% (the lower bound) keeps the energy savings estimate conservative.

The 30% efficiency gain on cooling energy represents the reduction achievable when waste heat recovery reduces the thermal load on mechanical cooling systems — a documented outcome in heat reuse integration projects.
12
Data Center Knowledge (2025) — "Cooling systems account for roughly 30% to 40% of total energy consumption." datacenterknowledge.com
13
Uptime Institute via HighJoule (2022) — "Cooling accounts for 30–40% of total data center energy consumption." highjoule.com
14
Intelligent CIO (2026) — "Data center operators routinely allocate 25–40% of total facility power to cooling, with legacy air-cooled environments clustering around 30%." intelligentcio.com
Solid — lower bound of documented 30–40% range
